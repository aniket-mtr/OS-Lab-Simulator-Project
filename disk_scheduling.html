DISK SCHEDULING

SSTF(Shortest Seek Time First)

The SSTF disk scheduling algorithm minimizes seek time by servicing the request closest to the current position of the disk arm first. It calculates the seek time for each request by measuring the distance between the current position and the requested track. The request with the shortest seek time is then selected and serviced next. This approach aims to reduce the overall time spent moving the disk arm, potentially improving disk performance. However, it may lead to starvation of requests located farther away from the current position

FCFS(First Come First Serve)

The FCFS disk scheduling algorithm services disk requests in the order they arrive, without considering the distance between requests or the current position of the disk arm. Requests are serviced sequentially from the beginning of the queue to the end. While simple to implement, FCFS may result in longer seek times, especially if there are requests scattered across the disk. This can lead to inefficient disk utilization and poor overall performance.

SCAN

The SCAN disk scheduling algorithm services requests by moving the disk arm in one direction (e.g., towards the outermost or innermost track) until it reaches the end of the disk, then reverses direction. Requests are serviced in the order they are encountered while scanning back and forth. SCAN minimizes seek time by serving requests efficiently along the path of the disk arm, reducing unnecessary movement compared to FCFS. However, it can lead to longer wait times for requests located at the extremes of the disk if there is continuous high disk activity.

C-SCAN

The C-SCAN (Circular SCAN) disk scheduling algorithm is similar to SCAN but differs in how it handles the end of the disk. Instead of reversing direction at the end, C-SCAN wraps around to the beginning of the disk, forming a circular path. This prevents excessive waiting times for requests near the end of the disk, as they are promptly serviced when the disk arm returns to the beginning. C-SCAN is particularly useful for systems with high disk activity, as it ensures more equitable servicing of requests across the disk surface compared to SCAN.

LOOK

The LOOK disk scheduling algorithm is similar to SCAN but differs in its handling of requests at the end of the disk. Instead of moving the disk arm to the end of the disk and reversing direction, LOOK stops at the last request in the current direction and then reverses direction. This prevents unnecessary movement to the end of the disk and back, reducing seek time. LOOK serves requests along the path of the disk arm without scanning beyond the last request in each direction, resulting in potentially improved performance compared to SCAN.

C-LOOK

The C-LOOK (Circular LOOK) disk scheduling algorithm is an improvement over LOOK, focusing on reducing seek time by eliminating unnecessary movement at the ends of the disk. Similar to C-SCAN, C-LOOK wraps around to the beginning of the disk after servicing requests in one direction, forming a circular path. It services requests only up to the last request in each direction, avoiding unnecessary scanning to the end of the disk. C-LOOK aims to provide more efficient disk utilization and reduced seek time compared to LOOK, particularly in scenarios with high disk activity and varying seek distances.
